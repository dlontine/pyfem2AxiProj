4.2.2. Element formulation¶

The two-node elastic bar element is shown embedded in a two-dimensional space in Figure [fig:ch1.link]. In the element’s local coordinates (\(\tilde{\pmb{x}}_i\)), forces and displacements act along only the local \(\tilde{x}\) direction. When viewed in the global coordinates, forces and displacements act along all \(\pmb{x}_i\) coordinate directions. Thus, in two dimensions, the bar element has \(2\) degrees of freedom per node, for a total of \(4\) degrees of freedom. Elements of this type are referred to as plane bar elements and a system made up of plane bar elements is a plane truss. In three dimensions, the element has \(3\) degrees of freedom per node, for a total of 6 degrees of freedom and is referred to as space bar elements. A system made up of space bar elements is a space truss.

Two-node elastic bar element

In three dimensions, the element node displacement and force vectors in three dimensions are arranged as

\[\begin{split}\boldsymbol{u}-{(e)} = \begin{Bmatrix} u_{1x} \\ u_{1y} \\ u_{1z} \\ u_{2x} \\ u_{2y} \\ u_{2z} \end{Bmatrix}, \quad \boldsymbol{f}-{(e)} = \begin{Bmatrix} f_{1x} \\ f_{1y} \\ f_{1z} \\ f_{2x} \\ f_{2y} \\ f_{2z} \end{Bmatrix}\end{split}\]

The displacement and force vectors in the global coordinates are related to their local counterparts through the following transformations

\[\tilde{\boldsymbol{u}}-{(e)} = \boldsymbol{T}\boldsymbol{u}-{(e)}, \quad \tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}-{(e)} = \boldsymbol{T}\boldsymbol{f}-{(e)},\]

where \(\boldsymbol{T}\) is the \(2 \times 6\) matrix of direction cosines of the axial axis of the element, given by

\[\begin{split}\label{eq:transm} \boldsymbol{T} = \begin{bmatrix} l_{ij} & m_{ij} & n_{ij} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & l_{ij} & m_{ij} & n_{ij} \\ \end{bmatrix}, \quad\end{split}\]

The direction cosines \(l_{ij}\), \(m_{ij}\), and \(n_{ij}\) are given by

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \label{eq:dircos} l_{ij} &= \cos\left(\tilde{x}, x\right) = \frac{x_j-x_i}{l_e} \\ m_{ij} &= \cos\left(\tilde{x}, y\right) = \frac{y_j-y_i}{l_e} \\ n_{ij} &= \cos\left(\tilde{x}, y\right) = \frac{z_j-z_i}{l_e}\end{aligned}\end{split}\]

The force-displacement relationship in the local coordinates is

\[\tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}-{(e)} = \tilde{\boldsymbol{k}}-{(e)}\tilde{\boldsymbol{u}}-{(e)}\]

where \(\tilde{\boldsymbol{k}}-{(e)}\) is the \(2 \times 2\) element stiffness matrix in the local coordinates. Using the coordinate transformations, the force-displacement relationship in the global coordinates is

\[\label{eq:ch1.force-disp-1} \tilde{\boldsymbol{f}}-{(e)} = \boldsymbol{T}\boldsymbol{f}-{(e)} = \tilde{\boldsymbol{k}}-{(e)}\tilde{\boldsymbol{u}}-{(e)} = \tilde{\boldsymbol{k}}-{(e)}\boldsymbol{T}\boldsymbol{u}-{(e)}\]

From , we get

\[\boldsymbol{f}-{(e)} = \boldsymbol{k}-{(e)}\boldsymbol{u}-{(e)}\]

where the element stiffness matrix in the global coordinates \(\boldsymbol{k}-{(e)}\) is

\[\label{eq:gstiff1} \boldsymbol{k}-{(e)} = \boldsymbol{T}-T\tilde{\boldsymbol{k}}-{(e)}\boldsymbol{T}\]

Expanding in components, the force-displacement relationship can be expressed as

\[\begin{split}\begin{Bmatrix} \boldsymbol{f}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{f}_2 \end{Bmatrix} = \frac{AE}{l_e} \begin{bmatrix*}[r] \boldsymbol{n}\otimes\boldsymbol{n} & -\boldsymbol{n}\otimes\boldsymbol{n} \\ -\boldsymbol{n}\otimes\boldsymbol{n} & \boldsymbol{n}\otimes\boldsymbol{n} \\ \end{bmatrix*} \begin{Bmatrix} \boldsymbol{u}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{u}_2 \end{Bmatrix}\end{split}\]

where the vectors \(\boldsymbol{f}_i\) and \(\boldsymbol{u}_i\) are the force and displacement vectors for the \(i-{\rm th}\) node in the global coordinates, \(A\) and \(E\) are the constant element area and Young’s modulus, respectively, \(l_e\) is the element length, and \(\boldsymbol{n}\) is the element unit normal, given by

\[\begin{split}\begin{aligned} \boldsymbol{v} &= \boldsymbol{x}_2-\boldsymbol{x}_1 \\ \boldsymbol{n} &= \frac{\boldsymbol{v}}{\lVert\boldsymbol{v}\rVert}\end{aligned}\end{split}\]

The product \(\boldsymbol{a}\otimes\boldsymbol{b}\) represents the vector outer product whose result is a matrix. In direct, indicial, and matrix notation, the matrix \(\boldsymbol{A}\) resulting from the outer product of vectors \(\boldsymbol{a}\) and \(\boldsymbol{b}\) is

\[\begin{split}\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{a}\otimes\boldsymbol{b} = a_i b_j = \begin{bmatrix} a_1b_1 & a_1b_2 & \ldots & a_1b_n \\ a_2b_1 & a_2b_2 & \ldots & a_2b_n \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_nb_1 & a_nb_2 & \ldots & a_nb_n \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

4.2.3. Element stiffness function¶

The computation of stiffness matrix for the two-node elastic bar is performed in the function , given in Listing [lst:stiff1]. is invoked as

ke = Link2Stiffness(xc, E, A)

Arguments to the are

xc Nodal coordinates
E Element modulus.
A Element area.

The output from is

ke Element stiffness stored as a (numdim*2, numdim*2) symmetric matrix, where numdim is the number of degrees of freedom per node. 
def Link2Stiffness(xc, E, A):
    # Element dimensionality
    xc = asarray(xc)
    if xc.ndim == 1:
        numdim = 1
    else:
        numdim = xc.shape[1]
    # Compute element normal
    v = xc[1] - xc[0]
    h = sqrt(dot(v, v))
    n = v / h
    if xc.ndim == 1:
        nn = 1.
    else:
        nn = outer(n, n)
    # Assemble element stiffness
    k = zeros((2*numdim, 2*numdim))
    i, j = numdim, 2*numdim
    k = zeros((2*numdim, 2*numdim))
    k[0:i, 0:i] = k[i:j, i:j] =  nn # upper left and lower right 2x2
    k[0:i, i:j] = k[i:j, 0:i] = -nn # lower left and upper right 2x2
    return A * E / h * k
